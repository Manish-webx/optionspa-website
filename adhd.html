Understanding the Condition

A Different Way of Thinking

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects the executive functions of the brain—like focus, memory, and impulse control. It’s not about a lack of effort; it’s about how your brain prioritizes stimuli.

At Options Psychiatry, we move beyond just managing symptoms. We use a combination of diagnostic expertise and advanced treatment to help you align your brain’s natural strengths with the demands of your daily life.

Early Detection

Common Signs of ADHD in Adults

ADHD often presents differently in adulthood, manifesting as chronic disorganization, internal restlessness, or difficulty completing long-term projects.

Time Blindness

Struggling to estimate how long tasks will take or frequently being late.

Task Paralysis

Feeling overwhelmed by large projects and not knowing where to start.

Hyperfocus

Becoming so engrossed in interesting tasks that you lose track of everything else.

Internal Restlessness

Feeling a constant need to be busy or having "racing" thoughts that won't quiet down.

Clinical Presentation

How ADHD Affects You

ADHD typically manifests in three primary ways, depending on which executive functions are most affected.

Inattentiveness

Difficulty sustaining attention, making "careless" mistakes, and losing things frequently.

Hyperactivity

Excessive movement, inability to sit still for long, and a constant feeling of being "on the go."

Impulsivity

Acting without thinking, interrupting others, and struggling with patience or delayed gratification.

Clinical Categories

Types of ADHD

Understanding which presentation you have is essential for choosing the right treatment strategies.

Predominantly Inattentive

Characterized by significant difficulty with attention and focus, but with little to no hyperactivity.

Predominantly Hyperactive

Characterized by high levels of energy, impulsivity, and physical movement, with less impact on focus.

Combined Presentation

The most common type, where individuals experience significant symptoms of both inattention and hyperactivity.
